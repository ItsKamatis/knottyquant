<section id="overview">

<h2>Overview</h2>

The project follows a dated USD SOFR workflow from market inputs to book-level valuation and risk. It deliberately uses one curve, one convention set, and a small synthetic trade book so that calibration, cash-flow generation, revaluation, and attribution can be inspected together.

<div class="result-grid">

  <div><span>Closing book</span><strong>3 swaps / &#36;47m gross notional</strong></div>
  <div><span>Closing model value</span><strong>−&#36;55,489.98</strong></div>
  <div><span>Parallel DV01</span><strong>about &#36;4,439.54 per bp</strong></div>
</div>

The output is a research implementation, not an executable market curve or a production control system. The daily bridge is described as a **value-change explain**, because the sample does not contain independent entry consideration or an accounting P&amp;L record.

</section>

<h2>Data and assumptions</h2>

The closing sample is dated 2 July 2026; the opening snapshot is 1 July. Thirteen synthetic OIS quotes span 1W through 30Y. The opening book has two swaps, and the closing book has three after the new trade is introduced.

The convention set is T+1 spot, annual fixed and floating payments, ACT/360, modified following, a two-USNY-business-day payment lag, linear interpolation in log discount factors, and no extrapolation beyond the final pillar.

<section id="method">

<h2>Method</h2>

Each OIS quote solves one new terminal discount factor while earlier pillars remain fixed. The root condition is

$$
q_i A_i(D) - PV_{\mathrm{float},i}(D)=0.
$$

Intermediate dates use log-linear discount-factor interpolation. Requests after the 30Y pillar fail explicitly rather than extending the curve silently.

<span id="swap" class="anchor-alias"></span>

### Swap valuation

Fixed and floating schedules are generated separately. A seasoned unpaid SOFR coupon combines realized daily fixings before the valuation date with forward discount-factor ratios over the remaining interval. Missing required fixings are errors rather than projected substitutes.

$$
C=\prod_i\left(1+r_i\frac{n_i}{360}\right),
\qquad
V=s\left(PV_{\mathrm{fixed}}-PV_{\mathrm{float}}\right).
$$

Parallel and key-rate DV01 use central quote bumps. Every bump rebuilds the complete curve and revalues every swap. The steepener and flattener apply opposing ±25 bp linear ramps between 1W and 30Y and also use full revaluation.

</section>

<section id="implementation">

<h2>Implementation</h2>

The bootstrap returns solved pillars and quote-repricing diagnostics. Risk functions store the original quote set, apply the requested perturbation, call the same bootstrap, and then pass the rebuilt curve to the swap pricer. There is no separate risk-only approximation path.

<CodeExcerpt
  label="src/sofr_curve/curve.py — one solved discount factor per quote"
  code={'def objective(log_df: float) -> float:\n    candidate = DiscountCurve(\n        as_of,\n        tuple(pillar_dates + [pillar]),\n        np.array(dfs + [math.exp(log_df)]),\n        curve_name,\n    )\n    annuity, floating_pv, _ = _ois_leg_values(\n        quote, candidate, calendars, conventions\n    )\n    return quote.rate * annuity - floating_pv\n\nroot, result = brentq(\n    objective, -20.0, math.log(3.0),\n    xtol=1e-14, rtol=1e-14, full_output=True,\n)'}
/>

The same rebuild contract is used for a one-basis-point quote bump, a key-rate bump, and a shaped curve scenario. This makes risk results depend on the same conventions and numerical solver as the closing valuation.

</section>
