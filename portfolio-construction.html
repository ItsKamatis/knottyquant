Constrained Portfolio Construction and Factor Attribution

A walk-forward comparison of portfolio construction methods under position, tracking-error, turnover, transaction-cost, factor-exposure, and exact-cardinality constraints.

Research question

Given noisy estimates, how do portfolio objectives and practical constraints change the weights, risk, turnover, and attribution that follow?

Data boundary: Public figures use a deterministic synthetic panel. A retained CRSP-derived price panel is evaluated locally without live WRDS access and is not published.

Overview

This project studies the layer between an estimated return distribution and a feasible portfolio. The focus is not a return forecast by itself, but the decisions that follow: covariance shrinkage, downside objectives, benchmark-relative risk, transaction costs, exposure controls, and discrete holding counts.

Public universe12 synthetic assets
Holding periods72 monthly decisions
Main comparison7 long-only methods
CardinalityExactly 8 holdings

The public run is an engineering demonstration. It is designed to expose method behavior and feasibility checks, not to establish alpha or an investable strategy.

Data and assumptions

The published demonstration uses deterministic synthetic asset and factor returns. Twelve assets are rebalanced monthly from January 2020 through December 2025 after a 2018–2019 estimation warm-up. Method development covers 2020–2023; 2024–2025 is already inspected and is labeled accordingly rather than called a pristine test.

Data boundary

The external adapter has been exercised on retained CRSP-derived price history without a live WRDS connection. Those rows, weights, and performance outputs are not distributed. The retained panel is split-adjusted price history without dividends and uses a current-constituent universe, so it carries a survivorship boundary.

Portfolio weights for month ttt use information dated through t−1t-1t−1. Missing returns are not replaced with zero. Before the next rebalance, prior weights drift with realized returns; turnover and costs are computed from those drifted weights.

Method

Expected returns use 50% shrinkage toward the cross-sectional grand mean. Covariance uses Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage. The comparison is deliberately curated: each method represents a distinct construction question rather than another solver configuration.

Equal weightNoneReference portfolio
Global minimum varianceMinimize varianceRisk-only baseline
Shrinkage mean–varianceReturn minus risk penaltyNoisy-forecast allocation
Maximum estimated SharpeBest solved frontier pointExplicit GMV fallback when excess return is nonpositive

Benchmark-relativeActive utility net of turnover penaltyTracking error, turnover, and factor controls
Exact-cardinality CVaRCVaR with binary selectionExactly eight holdings

The benchmark-relative default uses a 20% position cap, 8% ex-ante tracking-error limit, 30% one-way turnover limit, active Fama–French five-factor plus momentum controls, and a 10 bp one-way transaction-cost model. The cardinality formulation uses 1%–25% weights when selected.

The 130/30 formulation is kept as a mathematical appendix rather than included in the main comparison because the project does not model borrow availability, fees, recalls, financing, margin, or market impact.

Implementation

The continuous problems are expressed in CVXPY. A benchmark-relative weight vector is constrained against the eligible-universe benchmark, prior drifted weights, the covariance estimate, and lagged factor exposures.

Exact-cardinality CVaR is a mixed-integer linear program solved through SciPy’s HiGHS interface. Binary selection variables are linked to continuous weights, and the selected count is fixed before the loss-slack constraints are added.

Results and checks

The synthetic paths show how construction rules trade return, volatility, and turnover under the same dated panel and cost model. They are not interpreted as market performance.

Lagged rolling asset regressions estimate Fama–French five-factor plus momentum exposures. Monthly active return is reconciled into factor contributions, a specific residual, and transaction costs.

Every solution is checked against its stated budget, bound, tracking-error, turnover, exposure, and integrality conditions. The maximum-Sharpe rule used its documented GMV fallback in 19 of 72 months, and the exact-cardinality method selected eight assets in every published month.

A six-month moving-block bootstrap reports 95% intervals for return, volatility, Sharpe ratio, and maximum drawdown. Every public-demo Sharpe interval crosses zero, so the project makes no statistical-performance claim.

Limitations and materials

Public evidence

Displayed performance and attribution use synthetic assets and factors. They demonstrate the pipeline, not an empirical strategy.

Retained data

The local price panel omits dividends and uses a current-constituent universe, creating survivorship and total-return limitations.

Tail estimation

Private CVaR windows contain 18–29 complete monthly scenarios, often leaving only one or two observations in the 95% tail.

Coverage

Historical sector classifications and point-in-time benchmark weights are unavailable, so sector constraints and Brinson attribution are omitted.

The Github repository, Portfolio summary, bootstrap intervals, monthly results, and active-return attribution provide the public demonstration record.
